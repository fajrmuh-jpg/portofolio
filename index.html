Developing a Bill of Quantities

Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) Identification

A project is decomposed into smaller, manageable components called work packages through a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS), which is defined as a hierarchical, deliverable oriented framework where the total project scope is established to bridge project goals with detailed plans, ensuring that all deliverables, costs, and resources are accurately identified and scheduled.

Quantity Takeoff (QTO)

is the most critical phase in the pre construction stage where an estimator or Quantity Surveyor (QS) deconstructs every element within the technical drawings (architectural, structural, and MEP) to identify and measure every physical unit of material to be purchased and labor to be hired. This process is not merely counting items but a specialized analytical technique that transforms design visualizations into highly accurate raw numerical data; this data then serves as the "backbone" for developing the Bill of Quantities (BoQ), scheduling material procurement to prevent onsite surpluses or shortages, and acting as a control tool to monitor material wastage throughout the project duration.

Basic Rate Analysis (Resources Pricing)

Once the quantities are finalized, you must determine the current market rates for the project’s location. Labor: Standard wage rates based on regional regulations or market averages. Material: Purchase price of materials delivered to the site (including logistics and handling). Equipment: Hourly or daily rental rates for machinery and specialized tools.

Unit Price Analysis (UPA)

This step involves merging the quantities with production coefficients. In international practice, this is often called Resource-Based Estimating. Calculate the "Coefficient" (the amount of labor, material, or equipment time needed to complete one unit of work). Example: To calculate the unit price for 1 m³ of excavation, you multiply the hourly labor rate by the productivity index required to move that volume of soil.

Recapitulation & Final Reporting

The final step is consolidating all data into a Bill of Quantities (BoQ) spreadsheet, featuring Item Descriptions, Units, Quantities, Unit Rates, and Total Prices, costs per work division, Tax (VAT) and a calculated margin for overhead and profit (typically ranging from 5% to 15%).
